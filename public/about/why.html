About the Site

or, a long winded tangent on why I made one to begin with

So everyone’s got a reason to make a website...what’s mine?

The first is simple: I needed a project. Without something new to engage me, I end up going a little stir-crazy then eventually depressed. I had been considering making one off-and-on for about a year before 2026 so it all ended up depending on when I decided to pull the trigger. I can happily confirm my mental health has improved with doing this!

As for the second...

I could wax poetic about the evils of social media and the downfall of individuality in the face of the corporate Internet. Plenty of people have and I feel the points I agree with have been touched on. Instead, I’m going to hone in on a specific piece of this topic; there is nowhere on the Internet that caters to artists.

Unfortunately, there are many more consumers of art on the Internet rather than artists and it’s just more profitable for companies to cater to the majority. As a result, to be an artist on the Internet means attempting to cater to the consumer. Making sure you post during “active hours”, adding the appropriate tags (or not!), being mindful of what words you use, posting enough, posting the right thing…

...it gets to be way too stressful.

And this doesn’t even touch on the spectre of AI art and AI “artists”. Now you have to deal with people who don’t even bother to respect authentic artists or art nor understand what the appeal of art is at all. Sure, generative AI makes making art easy but the thing is the process is just an enjoyable as the final product.

Anyway, remember DeviantArt? Not the site as it is now but the site as it was in the early 2000s/2010s. That was a site for artists, where personalization was encouraged and the young artist could cut their teeth on sharing their art.

Then Eclipse happened. Then the embracing of AI happened. Then the rampant commercialization happened.

(And that’s not even taking account where their parent company is from.)

In the wake of DeviantArt’s fall from grace, artists spread out. Tumblr is a decent place; with its chronological feed and tags but the NSFW ban scared away a lot of artists. Twitter (or X, if you prefer that—which I don’t) might have been alright once upon a time but now, thanks to an increasingly hostile upper management and a culture that emphasizes negativity, it’s not at all kind to artists. The less said about Instagram the better.

Many sites have tried to fill the void but I don’t think any have quite captured the same magic early 2000s DeviantArt did—nor the popularity. A site is nothing if you can’t attract users.

Of course, being here on the indie web doesn’t guarantee people will see my content. Which is fine! I’m far past caring about attention and views. I can have enough fun kitting my site out with the features I want and never have to worry about things changing suddenly. And this way I guarantee anyone who does come across my stuff will be approaching it as genuinely as can be.
